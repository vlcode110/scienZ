Black body radiation

the ultraviolte catastrophe and the dawn of quantum mechanics

Feynman was a really humorous guy, constantly joked and seemed not to take anything seriously. Well, except for his work, except for physics. And he liked simplifying stuff. Thus, he made the most threatening mathematical theory accessible and understandable by introducing his Feynman diagrams. The diagrams are a set of lines representing the motion of electrons and their exchange of photons. The diagrams represent mathematical expressions. They literarily show what is happening and how. Each part of the diagram corresponds to a mathematical term. Feynman developed a process to derive these expressions back and forth. These three events are: 1. Photon moves from one location to the other 2. Electron moves from one location to the other 3. Electron emits a photon or absorbs a photon

The exchanges of different quantities of photons have different probabilities. These probabilities could be expressed graphically as arrows. Imagine a clock. A probability amplitude (i.e. a complex number) is, quite simply, an arrow, with a direction and a length. Calculating these probabilities follows one simple rule. The probability is the square of the amplitude. We have arrows and surfaces now, instead of wave functions and absolute squares of complex numbers which they technically represent.

The way he combines these arrows make sense too. He even notes the difference between photons (bosons) and electrons (fermions): for bosons, we just add arrows; for fermions, we need to subtract them (see my post on amplitudes and statistics in this regard). The wave represents exchanged photon. Each vertex where electron and photon lines meet represents a point at which an interaction occurs and is equivalent to a numerical factor that denotes the strength of the electromagnetic interaction. The number of these factors in the resulting expression is equal to number of vertices in the diagram. By combining all these pieces, a physicist can take the process depicted in a diagram and reconstruct the full mathematical statement that would result from manipulating the QED equations.

These diagrams are equivalent to the classical physics of Maxwell, This is truly remarkable in itself, but there is much more to the Feynman diagrams. Crucially, the same recipe can be used to construct any Feynman diagram: simply identify each algebraic component with the corresponding diagrammatic component and clip them together to form the diagram. What is unpleasant, is that to get a result you still need to perform some calculations, and not trivial ones as well. But better than the ones in the original version. There can be multiple possible interactions between particle. That’s why the arrows vary in length which is meant to represent probability, so longest arrows make the largest contribution to the final answer.
