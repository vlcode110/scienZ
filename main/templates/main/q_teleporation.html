Quantum teleportation

or how teleporattion is the intrinsic quality of nature on the quantum level

There are three main theories of technical possibilities for teleportation: teleportation using wormholes, one concerning the transfer of matter in the form of energy in accordance with E=mc2 and one concerning the transfer of information about the incident body and further reassembling of the body. In this essay, I will focus of the latter.

Quantum teleportation is a way to transfer information about a quantum system from one location to the other, which potentially enables to transfer the data on how to reassemble the body using different atoms. Insofar, quantum teleportation has a larger potential for transferring data, rather than physical objects, because in the given circumstances a physical object is a collection of information on all particles in the object and represents a very complicated quantum system. Quantum teleportation of humans or massive complex systems remain scientific fiction, however, there has been a strong development in teleportation of individual particles, most notably photons, over large distances. The recent advancements in quantum teleportation can potentially be used for a new encryption system.

Identically to quantum computing, quantum teleportation uses a qubit as a unit of information. A qubit is the smallest unit of information in the quantum world. It is the analogue to a bit, however, unlike its classical counterpart, can take two values simultaneously instead of one. A bit can be equal to either 1 or 0, while a qubit can be equal to |0⟩|0⟩, |1⟩|1⟩, |0⟩|1⟩ or |1⟩|0⟩. This is because quantum particles can be in multiple states simultaneously. Superposition of quantum states forms a new valid quantum state.

Another property of quantum systems which enables quantum teleportation of particles is known as quantum entanglement. A pair of particles is entangled when the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently of the quantum state of the other particle. The outcome of an individual measurement of the state of a particle is random, but he states of the two particles are correlated (regardless of the distance separating them).

For example, two particles are created in such a way that the total spin of the system is zero. If the spin of one of the particles is measured on a certain axis and found to be anticlockwise, then the spin of the other particle will be clockwise. There is no information exchange between the particles (because nothing can travel faster than the speed of light, and an information exchange would in that case be faster), so the state of the second particle does not adjust itself to the measured state of the second particle, but is originally in the appropriate state. However, the existing entanglement between the two particles does not enable using them as a means for information transfer as the individual readings cannot be predetermined, so it is not possible to exchange information in this fashion.

In quantum world, we technically do not transfer the matter of the body itself, but rather the information on how the same body can be reassembled it a different location. Because we cannot record the state of a quantum system without changing it, the original quantum system is going to lose its original form. This is called the no-cloning theorem. It conveniently implies that the only valid copy would be the teleported one. Transfer of quantum state can be referred to as teleportation because the operation results in the qubit existing in the final location but not existing in the initial one.

Most of the scientific evidence for quantum teleportation insofar has been based on experiments with photons. Recent researches in University of Rochester and Purdue University have discovered that teleportation is also possible between electrons, which is important because electrons, as opposed to photons, have mass. In order to demonstrate quantum teleportation using electrons, the researchers used a technique based on the principles of Heisenberg exchange coupling. It employs the particles magnetic moment. If certain kinds of particles have the same magnetic moment, they cannot be in the same place at the same time. The researchers used the technique to distribute entangled pairs of electrons and teleport their spin states[3].
