Heisenberg's uncertainty

the fundamental reason why we can never know everything

The principle of uncertainty was discovered by Verner Heisenberg. It states that we cannot know the position of the particle and its velocity (well, actually, its momentum, but since momentum is mass times velocity, and we know the mass of a particle, it is the velocity) simultaneously. This is the most annoying thing in quantum mechanics, because this is this is this one annoying bit which forbids us from teleportation. However, it can as well be regarded as a purely technical problem: we just haven’t figured out the alternative ways to deal with this problem yet.

ΔxΔp h/4pi Δx is the uncertainty in position, Δp is the uncertainty in momentum you can know the position of an electron with a high degree of certainty, but not its momentum and vice versa because the product of two uncertainties must be greater of equal to some number. They are in the inverse proportion. If you increase one, you decrease the other, or, in this case, rather, if you decrease one, you increase the other. The more accurately you know the position of the particle, the less accurately you know the momentum and vice versa.
