Atomic models

a charsh course through our changing perspectives on the way nature is built

Today it seems pretty obvious that all matter, which means, everything, including such intangible things as air, consist of atoms. But it wasn’t always like that. If we dive into a bit of history, back to Ancient Greece, we will find that the debates about elementary particles have been going on even back then. The most famous Greek philosopher Aristotle had a very fun theory that all matter consists of different mixes of the four elements (air, water, fire, earth). Now we know that every single one of these “elements” itself consists of atoms. But back then it was far from being obvious.

However absurd the previous theory sounds, the origin of atomic theory actually does date back to ancient Greece. Specifically, to the fifth century BC natural philosopher Democritus. Alongside his teacher Leuccipus, he was the first one to suggest that matter consisted of smaller particles. They never perceived atoms as something too special, just understood that if you cut matter enough many times, eventually, you will reach a particle that can’t be cut anymore. “Atom” = uncuttable.

The next major insight belonged to John Dalton. He stated that atoms existed as discrete packets of matter. Further investigations with sa cathode tube by JJ Thompson heled to discover the electron. He didn’t exactly know what they were, he only knew that they were negatively charged, had mass of about 0.001 of the mass of hydrogen (the smallest known unit of matter at the time). The atoms are overall are neutral, so there were also positive particles to balance the negative ones out. This gave birth to the Thompson plum-pudding model of atom. This model implies that the negative particles are randomly distributed throughout the atom and floating in positively charged matrix like fruit bits in a pudding. Sounds insane, but that was the first model of an atom which had truth to it.

Rutherford made the next breakthrough with his famous gold-foil experiment. He bombarded a piece of gold foil with alpha particles. The expectation was that the particles will just pass through if the pudding model was correct. This would have happened if the positive charges were scattered throughout the matter equally. Most of the particles did pass through, but some tiny bit of them got deflected at large angles. A tiny bit, about 1/8400, but it mattered. This implies that all the positive charge is concentrated at a single location inside an atom, and this location is very-very small. This is how the nucleus was discovered. More than that, he also correctly concluded that most of the atom was empty space.

Neils Bohr is the superhero of quantum mechanics. His model of the atom is the famous planetary model. Besides, he discovered the Bohr’s principle of quantization of energy. Based on the proportions of atoms which got deflected and the angles at which they did it, Bohr predicted the most likely location of electrons and constructed the model. But this is exactly the point. He constructed it based on the most LIKELY positions. These orbitals that we tend to expect to find the atom on is the most probable, not the ultimate location of an atom. Besides, the atoms isn’t a 2D thing, but is very much 3D (at least).The electron has a certain probability to be found in any point within the probability cloud. This is today’s convention, the best way we can visualize the atom to date: the cloud model.

We have been told a million times that the atoms consist of a nucleus of protons and neutrons with electrons orbiting them. This is true, but a very simplified one. Today’s prime convention in particle physics is the standard model. The particles are generally divided into two categories: the leptons and the quarks. The leptons are those light particles like the electrons and its heavier alike types: tau and muon. They come alongside their neutrinos. Back in September, I made a talk about neutrinos. Now you can see where they are in the standard model and what they are actually doing there. Notice, that neither protons, nor neutrons are here. This is because they are not elementary particles. They consist of quarks. This is called quark confinement.

So, there are 6 flavors of quarks. They normally come in pairs. The up-down ones are those that matter consists of. The flavors of quarks vary in size and mass, with the up and down ones being the lightest, and the top and bottom ones being the heaviest. The strange one. I should note that the strange quark has strangeness of -1 and the strange antiquark - of +1.

Quarks never exist on their own. They only exist in groups of 2 or 3. This is because they have partial values of spin. I will explain spin in one of the following sessions, but for now I will just say that it is something that can only come in certain packages, as energy cannot come in non-quantized packages. See, all about discrete values. `Moving a quark away from its neighbors requires energy. This energy gets stored in the baryon or meson that is being divided. But, since energy is discrete, the more and more energy gets provided into the separation, the quarks will be produced!!!!!! Antiparticles also belong to this group. Baryons include the familiar proton, consisting of 2 up quarks and 1 down quark, and its evil twin: the anti-proton, consisting of 2 up antiquarks and 1 down antiquark. They also include neutron, consisting of 2 down quarks and 1 up quark. Antineutron exists as well. It has charge 0 and consists of 1 down antiquarks and one up antiquarks. The group also includes lambda, a zero charge particle which contains a strange quark. In all the rest, it is alike a neutron. But the fact that one of its down quark has been replaced with a strange quark makes it a strange particle with strangeness of -1. Of baryons, only proton and neutron are stable. Omega particle consists of three strange particles.It is very heavy for a subatomic particle and has a charge of -1.

Nothing of normal matter consists of these particles. They were discovered in the experiments on the large hadron collider. They appear in interactions of other particles and live for fractions of a second. Lambda, for example, lives for 10 to the power of -10 seconds. It might seem very short, but it was initially predicted to liver for 10 to the power of -23 second. It is its strangeness that caused it to live so long, and this is how this property was discovered in the first place.

Mesons are the particles that consist of two quarks. More specifically, of one quark and one antiquark. Mesons are very unstable particles, and what it means is they have a very short lifespan. Some of the mesons are actually exchange particles, like the pion. The mesons are called like that because their mass is between the masses of a proton and an electron. “Mesa” in some of the Latin languages is translated as “middle”.
