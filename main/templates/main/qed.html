Symmetry is one of the fundamental laws of nature, or rather it is incorporated in the most fundamental laws of nature. An object or a pattern possesses symmetry if it remains completely unchanged after it is transformed in some way. For example, there are multiple laws of symmetry, or invariance, as it scientifically called, such as the law of conservation of energy, the law of conservation of charge, the law of conservation of spin (one of today’s subtopics), the law of conservation of momentum.

The link between laws of conservation and symmetry was discovered by Emmy Noether in the 20th century after studying Einstein’s relativity. Invariance also implies that the laws of physics do not change with place and time. Other examples include snowflakes. There is a mathematical study of symmetry called group theory. Symmetry has been one of the main focuses of early scientists such as Euclid, Pythagoras, Plato and other fathers of math and geometry. They all were convinced that the rules the Universe obeys must be symmetric. It is still considered that the deepest and most primary laws of nature should have beautiful.

In QED, in a field the electrons exchange virtual photons. Sound difficult, but it is really not. You see, what we should find in a field is energy. In QED, energy can be represented as particles exchanging photons. An electron randomly emits a photon, and another electron absorbs it. Thus, the first electron has less energy and the second one – more. But this is only briefly, because at the next fraction of a second some different photon would pass on an electron to it.

The idea of this photon being “virtual” is actually just that it exists very briefly, essentially solely for the exchange. And obviously, as we are dealing with photons, the transitions happen at the speed of light. So, to understand the quantum field theory, the physicists needed to relate relativity and quantum mechanics somehow. Schrodinger’s equation explained everything in the known physics but relativity. QED does not go as broad because it only applies for particles with spin equal to ½, but it related the two adversary fields of science together. The particle spin does not mean spin as we would imagine it, not like a ballerina. Because the electrons are so small, of diameter 10^-18, to achieve the angular momentum expected from them they would have to move at the speed 10^12, which is impossible, as it is faster than the speed of light. So, it is much easier to accept they are not spinning at all. Spin is an intrinsic property of a particle. It does not define what the electron is doing, it is as unchangeable for a particle, as is mass. The particle creates a magnetic field around itself because it has charge and hence the electromagnetic induction enters the game. When a particle beam passes through a dipole magnet, it gets divided into two separate beams. One is spin up, the other is spin down. As they should sum up to 1, to -1, or to zero, the particles from each of the two groups would be called “spin ½ particles”, but up and down ae just wordings, that has nothing to do with the direction, in the same way as it was for quarks. As there are only two options, the particles would be either +1/2 or -1/2. Never zero.
Spin ½ particles are those like electrons and muons and tau.When a particle passes through a thing called Stern Gerlach apparatus, it can be orientated more up or down in terms of its magnetic poles. The reason why we can’t say that the electron is spinning and thus producing the magnetic field is because of the neutron, The neutron has spin, but it does not have charge. It can’t produce the magnetic field without a charge. Spin 1 particles get divided into three groups. These are nucleons, like protons and neutrons. Spin 3/2 particle – into 4 groups. Spin 2 – into 5 groups. So, when a neutron decays into a proton and an electron, their combined spin is equal to 1. But the spin of the original system was ½. To get it back into being a half-integer we need another fermion, another particle with spin equal to ½. That’s how we know there is a neutrino.
