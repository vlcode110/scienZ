Bremsstrahlung radiation

the curious effect that takes place when free electrons enter an atom

When high speed electrons are fired towards a target, they possess a certain amount of kinetic energy (Ek). As they enter the vicinity of a nucleus, the electrostatic attraction between the electron and the protons results in a deflection in the motion of the particle. The deceleration is equivalent to a loss in kinetic energy, which causes the electron to emit the lost portion of its energy in the form of a photon called Bremsstrahlung or "braking" Radiation.

The factors that influence the energy of the emitted photons are the following: 1. Initial energy provided to the oncoming particle - with higher kinetic energies resulting in increased frequencies of produced photons. 2. Degree of deflection of the electron - a greater change in the trajectory of the particle will result in a higher energy photon being released. 3. Density of nuclei in the target material - increases the potential for interactions between the nuclei and oncoming electrons. Due to a wide range of influencing factors, a distribution of of Bremsstrahlung wavelength’s (or Energies) will be formed.

Different elements can emit waves of different frequencies. You might have come across an expression “the emission spectrum of an element”. The most common formula utilised in the description of the spectral distribution of Bremstrahlung Radiation is the formula called Kramer’s Law. The value of λ0 corresponds to the highest possible energy (E0) of the emitted photon which is formed in the event that the electron collides with the nucleus directly (hence losing all of its kinetic energy). The constant of proportionality K is an experimental constant, which will be determined as a result of the experiment, however it is dependent on a myriad of factors such as: sample absorption of the observed radiation, the losses due to back scattering of electrons, the detection efficiency for energy E and geometric constants of detection.

As a consequence of Bremsstrahlung radiation, characteristic radiation is commonly observed. Charac- teristic radiation consists of X-rays formed when electrons of heavy elements undergo quantum transmission to lower energy levels ( Figure 3).
